Certainly, How long is she going to stay in london?​

About a fortnight I think.

Oh thank you. that's very kind of you I am sure yvunee will be please to meet you.

Oh they are both prettier than I am. Ruth is the prettiest girl I know. They both have long fair hair; but Ruth's hair is longer and fairer than Gretchen's. Gretchen is fatter than ruth. (She doesn't like you to say She is fat; and we tell her she will get thinner when she gets older.)

Comparison of adjectives (I)
In English, there are three degrees of comparison:

1- In lesson 20 you had sentences like these:
      - Hans is as old as Peter.
      - Peter is as tall as hans
      - They are as tall as each other.
This form of the adjective (old, tall, etc.) is called the Positive Degree.

2- Then you had sentences like these:
      - I am luckier than you.
      - Are Marie and Yvonne older than you?
      - Ruth's hair is longer than Gretchen's, etc.
This form of the adjective (luckier, older, younger, taller etc.) is called the Comparative Degree.
To form the comparative degree we add -er to the positive e.g.


Positive Comparative Positive Comparative
old older fat fatter1
tall taller lucky luckier2
big bigger1 pretty prettier2
thin thinner1 happy happier2

Note:
      1 When the adjective ends in a single consonant letter with a single vowel letter before it this consonant letter is double in writing.
      2 When the adjective ends in a consonant + y the y is changed to i

3- You also had a sentences like these:
      - I am the oldest of the family.
      - The youngest and the smallest one is Fritz.
      - Ruth is the prettiest girl I know.
      - They are the noisiest boys I know and the nicest.
* This form of the adjective is called the superlative degree
To form the superlative degree we add -est to the positive degree, e.g.

Positive Comparative Superlative
quiet quieter quietest
old older oldest
young younger youngest
tall taller tallest
fat fatter fattest
thin thinner thinnest
noisy noisier noisiest
prettey prettier prettiest

Notice particularly:

I- With the positive degree we use "as....as"
for example:
- Peter is as tall as Hans.
- Boys are not as quiet as1 Girls.
- A man is as old as he feels.
1 sometimes with a negative we use so...as. E.g., Frits is not so old as Hans.

II- With the comparative we use "than"
for example:
- Frieda is older than Fritz.
- Boys are noisier than girls.
- Gretchen is fatter than Ruth.

With the superlative we often use "of" and "the"
for example:
Frieda is the oldest of them all.
Olaf is the tallest of Mr. Priestley's students.

Verbal nouns or gerund

1/ What is Gerund?
In English grammar a gerund (jer-ənd) is the verb with an “-ing” ending that fuction as a noun in a sentence.
Gerunds are often confused with present participle phrases because both of these parts of speech take an -ing form. However, gerunds always function as verbal nouns or noun phrases (e.g., “He likes running”), whereas present participles function as a verb phrase (e.g., “He is running”).
